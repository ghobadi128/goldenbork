Dog-Friendly Hiking Trails in the Bay Area

Updated May 2025·14 min read

Every trail listed here is one we've hiked with our own dogs. We note leash requirements, water availability, difficulty, and anything else that's actually useful to know before you go. Trail conditions change with weather and season — always check for closures before heading out.

Marin County Trails

Tennessee Valley Trail

Distance: 3.4 miles round trip · Difficulty: Easy · Dogs: On-leash required

This is the trail most Bay Area dog owners start with, and for good reason. It's flat, wide, and ends at a dramatic cove beach framed by cliffs. The path follows a creek for most of the way, and dogs can wade in shallow sections.

What we noticed: The main fire road is the easiest route — avoid the coastal trail spur if your dog doesn't handle narrow cliffsides well. Parking fills up by 10am on weekends and you'll need to park along the road. No trash cans on the trail — pack everything out. The beach has strong waves; don't let your dog swim in the surf.

Rodeo Beach Loop via Coastal Trail

Distance: 3.8 miles · Difficulty: Moderate (some elevation) · Dogs: Voice control areas, leash in others

Starting from the Rodeo Beach parking lot in the Marin Headlands, this loop climbs up to hill trails with views of the Pacific and the Golden Gate, then drops back down to the beach. The beach itself is pebbly and dog-friendly.

What we noticed: The uphill sections are steep enough to tire out an energetic dog. Some areas are designated voice-control (effectively off-leash if your dog is trained); others require leashes — signs are posted. Fog can roll in quickly and cut visibility. Bring layers and water.

Mt. Burdell Open Space — Novato

Distance: 5.4 miles round trip to summit · Difficulty: Moderate · Dogs: Off-leash on fire roads

Mt. Burdell is one of the few Marin County preserves that allows off-leash dogs on fire roads. The wide trails cross open grassland with wildflowers in spring, and the summit offers 360-degree views of the North Bay.

What we noticed: Very little shade — avoid midday in summer. Ticks are common in the grass, especially in spring. Check your dog thoroughly after the hike. The trail from the San Andreas Drive entrance is less crowded than the main trailhead. Cow grazing occurs in some areas — dogs must be leashed around livestock.

San Francisco Trails

Lands End Trail

Distance: 3.4 miles round trip · Difficulty: Moderate (rocky, uneven) · Dogs: On-leash

Lands End traces the rugged northwestern coastline of SF with views of the Marin Headlands and Golden Gate Bridge. The trail passes through cypress groves and along cliff edges with several viewpoints.

What we noticed: The path is rocky and uneven in sections — not ideal for dogs with joint issues. Keep your dog leashed and close on the cliff-side sections. The ruins of Sutro Baths at the western end are worth the stop. Parking at the Lands End Lookout visitor center fills fast on weekends — try the Merrie Way lot as backup.

East Bay Trails

Tilden Regional Park — Berkeley Hills

Distance: Varies (2–8 miles) · Difficulty: Easy to moderate · Dogs: On-leash on most trails

Tilden covers 2,079 acres in the hills above Berkeley with eucalyptus forests, open meadows, and Lake Anza (dogs can't swim in the lake, but the trails around it are pleasant).

What we noticed: The Nimitz Way trail along the ridgeline is flat, paved, and great for older dogs or those with mobility issues — plus the views of the bay are excellent. The Wildcat Creek area has more rugged terrain. Ticks are an issue here too, especially in spring.

Joaquin Miller Park — Oakland Hills

Distance: Varies · Difficulty: Easy to moderate · Dogs: Designated off-leash areas

A redwood forest in the Oakland Hills with quiet trails that feel far from the city. The off-leash areas are in the upper sections of the park along the fire roads.

What we noticed: The redwood groves stay cool even on warm days, making this a good summer option. The trails can be slippery after rain. The Sequoia-Bayview trail offers city and bay views through breaks in the trees. Parking is free at multiple lots.

Peninsula Trails

Mori Point — Pacifica

Distance: 2 miles round trip · Difficulty: Easy · Dogs: On-leash

A short coastal hike on the bluffs above Pacifica with stairs leading down to Rockaway Beach. The trail crosses open grassland with ocean views the entire way.

What we noticed: It's short enough for a quick after-work hike. The stairs are steep — some dogs handle them better than others. Whale watching is possible from the bluffs in winter months. Fog is frequent. Parking is free at the small lot off Bradford Way.

Rancho San Antonio — Cupertino

Distance: Up to 6 miles · Difficulty: Easy · Dogs: On-leash, county park sections only (no dogs in preserve)

A sprawling network of trails in the foothills above Cupertino. The lower loops are flat and wide — popular with dog walkers and runners. Note that dogs are only allowed in the county park portion, not the adjacent open space preserve.

What we noticed: The Deer Hollow Farm area is a nice destination if you stick to the county park trails. Very popular — the parking lot often hits capacity before 9am on weekends. Arrive early or try a weekday.

Trail Safety Notes

A few things we've learned from hiking with dogs in the Bay Area:

  • Ticks: Common from February through June, especially in tall grass. Check your dog's ears, neck, and between toes after every hike.
  • Foxtails: These barbed grass seeds appear in late spring and summer. They can embed in paws, ears, and noses. Avoid dry grass areas and inspect your dog after walks.
  • Rattlesnakes: Present on East Bay and Peninsula trails from spring through fall. Keep dogs on-trail and consider rattlesnake avoidance training.
  • Water: Most trails don't have water fountains. Carry enough for both you and your dog — at least 8oz per mile for your dog in warm weather.
  • Heat: Bay Area inland trails can reach 90°F+ in summer. Hike early or choose coastal/shaded trails. Pavement and exposed dirt burn paws.

Trail Conditions Change

We update this guide regularly, but conditions change with weather, seasonal closures, and fire risk. Always check local park district websites before heading out.
