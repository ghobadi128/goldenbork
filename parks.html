Best Dog Parks in the Bay Area

Updated May 2025·15 min read·By the GoldenBork team

We've visited every park on this list multiple times in different seasons and weather conditions. What follows is what we actually observed — not what a website said or a press release claimed. Hours and rules can change, so we recommend checking with your local parks department before a first visit.

San Francisco Parks

Fort Funston — Ocean Beach South

Fort Funston sits on coastal bluffs at the southern end of Ocean Beach. It's one of the most popular off-leash areas in the city, and for good reason — sandy trails wind along cliff tops with ocean views, and a steep sand ladder leads down to the beach itself.

What we noticed: The main loop trail is about 1.5 miles and stays mostly flat along the bluff. Wind is a constant — dress in layers even in summer. Erosion has closed some trails near the cliff edges; check the posted signs at the trailhead. The parking lot fills up by 10am on weekends.

Off-leash: Yes, in designated areas. Dogs must be under voice control.
Parking: Free lot off Skyline Blvd. Overflow along the road.
Water: No fountains. Bring your own. The ocean is accessible but has strong currents — watch your dog near the waterline.
Best time to go: Weekday mornings or late afternoons.

Crissy Field — Presidio

Crissy Field runs along the waterfront between the Marina and Fort Point, with the Golden Gate Bridge as a backdrop. The east beach area is a designated off-leash zone where dogs can run and wade in the bay.

What we noticed: The water here is calmer than the ocean side, making it safer for swimming dogs. The grassy areas near the warming hut are on-leash only. It gets crowded on sunny weekends, especially near the east beach. The paved path is shared with joggers and cyclists.

Off-leash: East Beach and the Wildlife Protection Area (when open). On-leash on paved paths.
Parking: Limited free lots. Street parking along Mason St.
Water: Water fountains available near the warming hut.
Best time: Early morning before the crowds arrive.

McLaren Park Dog Play Area

McLaren Park is San Francisco's second-largest park but gets a fraction of the visitors that Golden Gate Park does. The off-leash areas here feel more like a neighborhood spot — you'll see the same regulars most mornings.

What we noticed: The terrain is hilly with eucalyptus groves. Less polished than other parks but that's part of the appeal — it's quiet and spacious. Some trails are unpaved and get muddy after rain. The dog play area near the Jerry Garcia Amphitheatre is fenced.

Off-leash: Designated DPA areas. Check posted maps at entrances.
Parking: Free street parking along Shelley Drive.
Water: Limited. Bring your own.
Best time: Anytime — it rarely feels crowded.

Oakland Parks

Point Isabel Regional Shoreline — Richmond/El Cerrito

Point Isabel is one of the largest off-leash dog areas in the United States at 23 acres. It sits along the San Francisco Bay shoreline with views of the Golden Gate Bridge, Angel Island, and the SF skyline.

What we noticed: This is not a fenced park — it's open bayfront land with paved and dirt paths. Dogs can wade in the bay at several access points, though the mud can be intense at low tide. Mudpuppy's Tub & Scrub at the entrance offers dog washing if your pup gets filthy (and they will). The café there is solid for coffee.

Off-leash: The entire shoreline area is off-leash.
Parking: Large free lot. Fills up on weekend mornings.
Water: Dog water fountains throughout the park.
Best time: Weekday mornings. Weekend afternoons are packed.

Lake Merritt Dog Areas

Lake Merritt has three designated off-leash areas around the 3.4-mile lake path. They're fenced and separated by size — there's a small-dog section at the east end.

What we noticed: The areas are relatively small, so they work better for short play sessions than long runs. The east-side area near the bird sanctuary is usually the least crowded. The lake path itself is on-leash only and gets busy with joggers, especially before work hours.

Off-leash: Three designated fenced areas only.
Parking: Metered street parking around the lake.
Water: Fountains at each dog area.
Best time: Midday on weekdays.

Berkeley

César Chávez Park

César Chávez Park sits at the end of the Berkeley Marina on a 90-acre former landfill. It's now a wide-open grass-and-dirt park with panoramic bay views. The entire off-leash area is unfenced — dogs roam the hillsides freely.

What we noticed: The views from the hilltop are some of the best in the East Bay — you can see the Golden Gate, SF skyline, and Mount Tamalpais all at once. It's windy almost always. The ground is uneven with gopher holes, so watch your footing. Popular with kite flyers, which some dogs find very exciting.

Off-leash: Most of the park is off-leash. On-leash near the north waterfront meadow (nesting bird habitat).
Parking: Free lot at the marina.
Water: Bring your own — no dog fountains.
Best time: Late afternoon for the best light and fewer crowds.

Marin County

Remington Dog Park — Sausalito

A small but well-maintained fenced park in Sausalito with separate areas for large and small dogs. It's more of a neighborhood park than a destination, but useful if you're in the area.

What we noticed: The park has benches, shade trees, and water bowls. The ground is a mix of grass and decomposed granite. Regulars tend to come at the same times, so the vibe varies depending on when you go. Street parking is limited nearby.

San Jose

Hellyer County Dog Park

A large fenced dog park along the Coyote Creek Trail in South San Jose. It has separate sections for large and small dogs, shaded areas, and a decent amount of space to run.

What we noticed: This park gets hot in summer — there's limited tree cover in the main run area. The adjoining Coyote Creek Trail is a nice on-leash walk before or after. Water spigots are available. Parking requires a county parks pass ($6/day or annual pass).

Miyuki Dog Park — South San Jose

A newer fenced park with good surface drainage, which means it doesn't get as muddy as some other South Bay options. Separate small and large dog areas.

What we noticed: Well-lit for evening visits, which is uncommon for dog parks in the area. The small-dog area is genuinely useful — not just a token corner. Benches and shade structures throughout. Free parking.
